Full client brief
A family-history client is digitizing ~24 antique postcard photographs from 1910 documenting a famous stage magician and his traveling company touring Asia and abroad; the client's grandmother and aunt performed in the troupe and wrote a description of each scene on the BACK of the postcards. The client needs every postcard delivered as two separate restored files — the photographic FRONT as one picture and the handwritten BACK as another — that are clean enough to be pasted into a printed memorial book and posted to social media. Many of the front photos are dark, low-contrast, and tilted from flatbed scanning; the handwriting on the backs is faint cursive pencil/ink that is hard to read. This benchmark uses ONE representative postcard as the worked end-to-end example. The client supplies two raw flatbed scans: (1) the postcard front (a dark, slightly rotated sepia-toned albumen-style photograph of the magician and company on a theatre stage, with scanner-bed border) and (2) the postcard back (faded cursive handwriting plus a postmark and divided-back postcard printing, also slightly skewed). Required restoration pipeline, per side: ingest both raw scans into the workspace; auto-straighten each skewed scan and crop away the scanner-bed margins to the postcard edge; on the FRONT, recover the dark midtones and shadows, lift exposure and overall brightness/contrast, rein in any blown highlight on the bright stage lights, gently recover the crushed dark portions where the company stands in shadow, neutralize the scanner's cool cast and re-warm to a consistent archival sepia, and finish with a vintage-photo preset look that keeps the period feel while making faces and stage detail readable; on the BACK, push brightness and contrast hard and convert to a clean high-legibility monochrome/sepia so the faint cursive description reads clearly. Deliver a high-resolution restored FRONT and a high-resolution restored BACK, each deskewed and edge-cropped, color-consistent, copy-paste-ready for the book layout and for social posts. Separately document (out-of-band, from the parameters recorded during the run) the exact per-card operation sequence so the same recipe can be re-run across the remaining 23 postcards.
