Full client brief
We manage ~100 luxury vacation rentals in Saint-Martin and need a consistent, premium visual identity across the whole portfolio. Photos come out of a Nodalview HDR workflow already roughly exposed; your job is to enhance and FINALIZE them while keeping a natural, realistic look — never a fake-HDR, over-saturated, or unrealistically-blue-pool result. For this batch, finish one representative villa's three hero frames: (1) a luxury living-room interior shot through to a bright sea-view window, (2) a pool deck with turquoise water, and (3) a wide terrace looking out to sea and sky.

Required, in order: straighten any tilted horizon; apply a clean auto-tone baseline; do fine exposure and white-balance correction so the three frames read as one coherent set (no green/magenta casts, neutral whites indoors); lift interior shadows for brightness and clarity WITHOUT crushing the bright window view (balance the indoor/outdoor exposure); then do RESTRAINED, targeted enhancement of the exterior color story — pool water and sea color enhanced via selective hue/saturation of the cyans/blues only (not a global saturation pump), sky improved gently, vegetation greens enriched naturally. Keep overall vibrance gentle. Export all three at a matched 2048px long edge, sRGB JPEG q90, plus one 16:9 hero crop of the best exterior for the listing header. Finally assemble the finished frames into a single client review board so the property manager can approve the look before we roll it across the portfolio.

Out of connector scope and to be flagged for the human finisher: converging-vertical / perspective warp and any cable/reflection/stain cloning — the automated pass does everything else.

Why it's non-trivial
Long-horizon (19 connector calls, 13 distinct Adobe tools across the asset, photo-raster and board groups) with three parallel multi-step grade chains that must converge into one consistent look and one review board. Difficulty comes from ordering discipline (straighten→tone→WB→shadow/highlight balance must run in that order to be physically correct), from the restraint constraints (selective single-color saturation + gentle vibrance instead of a global pump to honor the explicit no-fake-HDR / no-over-saturation / no-unrealistic-blue-pool DO-NOT-WANT list), and from cross-frame consistency (the pool and terrace both use matched cyan/blue treatment so the set reads as one property). It is honestly a 'partial' grade: the connector does the entire repeatable HDR-finishing pass headless [C], but the brief's converging-vertical/perspective warp and occasional cable/stain cloning are out of connector scope and are explicitly handed to the human finisher rather than faked.
